Despite their success, these models processed sequences sequentially, limiting parallelization and making it difficult to capture long-distance dependencies.\n\n**2.3 Transformer-Based Architectures**\n\nThe transformer architecture, introduced by Vaswani et al. (2017), replaced recurrence with self-attention, enabling full parallelization and direct modeling of global context. This catalyzed a wave of pre-trained language models. BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) introduced bidirectional pre-training using masked language modeling and next-sentence prediction, achieving strong results on GLUE, SQuAD, and other benchmarks. Subsequent variants \u2014 RoBERTa, ALBERT, ELECTRA, DeBERTa \u2014 improved training efficiency and performance. Generative models such as GPT-2, GPT-3, and T5 demonstrated the power of large-scale autoregressive pre-training. Multilingual models (mBERT, XLM-R) extended capabilities to low-resource languages. The literature from 2018 to 2024 reflects a rapid succession of architectural innovations, scaling laws, and adaptation techniques.\n\n---\n\n**3. Methodology / System Design**\n\nWe conducted a systematic review of peer-reviewed literature published between 2018 and 2024. The search was performed across major databases including IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, ACL Anthology, and arXiv. Keywords included combinations of \"natural language processing,\" \"machine learning,\" \"deep learning,\" \"transformer,\" \"BERT,\" \"text classification,\" \"named entity recognition,\" \"machine translation,\" and \"question answering.\"\n\nInclusion criteria required that papers: (1) propose or evaluate a machine learning method for an NLP task; (2) report empirical results on a recognized benchmark; (3) be written in English. We excluded position papers, tutorials without novel evaluation, and works focusing solely on linguistic theory without computational modeling.\n\nThe review covers four major NLP tasks:\n- **Text Classification:** Sentiment analysis, topic categorization, intent detection.\n- **Named Entity Recognition:** Entity span detection and typing in general and domain-specific corpora.\n- **Machine Translation:** Bilingual and multilingual translation, including low-resource settings.\n- **Question Answering:** Extractive, generative, and multi-hop QA over text and knowledge bases.\n\nBoth supervised and unsupervised (or self-supervised) learning approaches were included. For each task, we tracked the evolution of model architectures, training objectives, data requirements, and reported performance metrics (accuracy, F1, BLEU, EM, etc.).\n\n---\n\n**4. Implementation**\n\nThe review was implemented as a structured literature analysis pipeline. Papers were collected, deduplicated, and screened by title and abstract. Full-text assessment was performed for 342 papers meeting initial criteria. Data extraction captured: publication year, venue, task, model architecture, training paradigm (supervised, self-supervised, few-shot), dataset(s) used, baseline comparisons, and key findings.\n\nA taxonomy was developed to categorize methods into: (1) traditional ML (pre-2018 baselines), (2) deep learning (RNN/CNN/attention-based, 2018\u20132020), (3) early transformers (BERT, GPT-2, 2019\u20132020), (4) optimized transformers (RoBERTa, ALBERT, T5, 2020\u20132022), and (5) large-scale and instruction-tuned models (GPT-3, LLaMA, Flan-T5, 2022\u20132024). This taxonomy guided the comparative analysis in Section 5.\n\n---\n\n**5. Results \\& Discussion**\n\n**5.1 Overall Trends**\n\nAcross all four tasks, transformer-based models consistently outperformed prior deep learning and traditional methods. The shift from task-specific architectures to pre-train-then-fine-tune paradigms reduced the need for task-specific engineering. Self-supervised pre-training on large unlabeled corpora proved more effective than supervised pre-training on intermediate tasks.\n\n**5.2 Text Classification**\n\nBERT and RoBERTa achieved near-saturation on standard benchmarks (e.g., SST-2, AG News, IMDB). Fine-tuning a pre-trained transformer with a classification head became the de facto standard. Lightweight variants (DistilBERT, TinyBERT) offered competitive accuracy with reduced latency. Few-shot prompting with large language models (LLMs) showed promise but remained sensitive to prompt design.\n\n**5.3 Named Entity Recognition**\n\nSequence labeling with transformer encoders + CRF decoding yielded state-of-the-art results on CoNLL-2003, OntoNotes, and domain-specific datasets (e.g., BC5CDR, JNLPBA). Contextual embeddings resolved ambiguity in entity boundaries and types. Cross-lingual transfer via mBERT and XLM-R enabled zero-shot NER in low-resource languages.\n\n**5.4 Machine Translation**\n\nTransformer-based encoder-decoder models (Vaswani et al., 2017) replaced RNN-based SMT and NMT. Scaling model and data size improved BLEU scores consistently. Multilingual models (M2M-100, NLLB) achieved competitive translation across 100+ languages. Unsupervised and semi-supervised methods leveraged back-translation and denoising objectives for low-resource pairs.\n\n**5.5 Question Answering**\n\nExtractive QA (SQuAD 1.1/2.0) was effectively solved by BERT-style models. Generative QA benefited from T5 and GPT-style pre-training. Multi-hop and open-domain QA required retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) and dense passage retrieval (DPR). Large LLMs demonstrated strong zero-shot QA but exhibited hallucination and factual inconsistency.\n\n**5.6 BERT and Variants**\n\nBERT's bidirectional context modeling was particularly effective for tasks requiring deep semantic understanding (NER, QA, natural language inference). Variants addressed limitations: RoBERTa removed next-sentence prediction and used dynamic masking; ALBERT reduced parameters via factorized embeddings and cross-layer sharing; ELECTRA replaced masked language modeling with replaced token detection; DeBERTa improved attention with disentangled mechanisms and enhanced mask decoding.\n\n**5.7 Remaining Challenges**\n\nDespite progress, key challenges persist: (1) **Efficiency** \u2014 large models require substantial compute for training and inference; (2) **Interpretability** \u2014 attention weights and probing classifiers offer limited causal insight; (3) **Low-resource languages** \u2014 performance gaps remain for languages with limited digital text; (4) **Robustness** \u2014 models are sensitive to distribution shift, adversarial examples, and spurious correlations; (5) **Factuality** \u2014 generative models hallucinate, especially in long-form QA.\n\n---\n\n**6. Conclusion**\n\nMachine learning continues to drive innovation in NLP. The field has progressed from feature-engineered pipelines to end-to-end deep learning, and most recently to large-scale pre-trained transformers that generalize across tasks. BERT and its variants demonstrated particular strength in understanding contextual relationships, establishing the pre-train-fine-tune paradigm as dominant. Generative LLMs have further expanded capabilities in open-ended generation and few-shot learning.\n\nFuture work should focus on three critical directions: **efficiency** \u2014 through distillation, quantization, sparse attention, and architecture search; **interpretability** \u2014 via mechanistic interpretability, causal analysis, and faithful explanations; and **handling low-resource languages** \u2014 through improved cross-lingual transfer, data augmentation, and community-driven resource creation. Addressing these will ensure that NLP technologies are accessible, trustworthy, and equitable.\n\n---\n\n**7.
