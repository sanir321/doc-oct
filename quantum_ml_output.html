Abstract —

This position paper compares three transformer architectures — BERT, Longformer, and BigBird — across standard-length text classification and long-document understanding tasks. BERT employs full self-attention with quadratic complexity, limiting its practical sequence length. Longformer introduces a combination of local windowed attention and global attention tokens to achieve linear scaling. BigBird adopts a sparse attention pattern combining random, local, and global attention, providing theoretical guarantees for approximating full attention. The analysis examines how these architectural choices affect representational capacity, computational efficiency, and task suitability, highlighting fundamental trade-offs between attention expressiveness and sequence length scalability.

Introduction

Transformer architectures have become the dominant paradigm in natural language processing, yet their quadratic attention complexity creates a fundamental bottleneck for long sequences. BERT established the pre-training and fine-tuning paradigm with full self-attention, but its 512-token limit restricts applicability to longer documents. Longformer and BigBird were developed to address this limitation through distinct sparse attention mechanisms. This paper provides a comparative analysis grounded in their architectural design principles, focusing on how attention pattern choices influence performance on standard-length classification versus long-document understanding. The comparison centres on the tension between attention expressiveness and computational tractability.

Architectural Comparison

BERT utilises full self-attention where every token attends to all other tokens, enabling rich pairwise interactions but incurring O(n²) memory and compute costs. This design maximises representational capacity for short sequences but becomes prohibitive beyond 512 tokens. Longformer replaces full attention with a sliding window mechanism where each token attends to a fixed local neighbourhood, supplemented by global attention tokens that attend to and are attended by all positions. This yields O(n) complexity while preserving task-specific global context through designated tokens such as [CLS]. BigBird employs a theoretically grounded sparse attention pattern combining three components: local windowed attention, global tokens, and random attention connections. The random component provides theoretical guarantees that the sparse pattern can approximate full attention and ensures connectivity across the sequence graph, supporting universal approximation properties.
The three architectures differ in how they allocate attention capacity. BERT distributes attention uniformly across all token pairs. Longformer uses a deterministic pattern with configurable window size and task-specific global tokens. BigBird introduces stochasticity through random attention, which varies per layer and head, potentially improving robustness and expressiveness. All three share the same underlying transformer block structure — multi-head attention, feed-forward networks, layer normalisation, and residual connections — differing primarily in the attention mask construction. These design choices reflect different philosophies: BERT prioritises maximum expressiveness within fixed length, Longformer emphasises predictable local structure with explicit global routing, and BigBird seeks theoretical completeness through randomised connectivity.

Task 1: Standard-Length Text Classification

For standard-length text classification tasks where sequences fit within BERT's 512-token limit, all three architectures can process inputs without truncation. BERT's full attention provides the most complete pairwise token interactions, which may benefit tasks requiring fine-grained relational reasoning across the entire sequence. Longformer's local windowed attention restricts direct interactions to nearby tokens, though global tokens can propagate information across the sequence. The effectiveness of this design depends on whether the classification signal relies predominantly on local syntactic patterns or long-range dependencies. BigBird's random attention introduces stochastic connectivity that, in expectation, approximates full attention, but the variance introduced by randomisation may affect fine-tuning stability on smaller datasets.
In practice, the choice among these models for standard-length tasks involves trade-offs between pre-training objectives, available checkpoints, and computational budget. BERT benefits from extensive pre-training corpora and mature fine-tuning recipes. Longformer and BigBird are typically initialised from checkpoints pre-trained with their respective sparse attention patterns, which may not transfer identically to short-sequence tasks. The local and random attention mechanisms were designed for long-sequence efficiency, not necessarily for optimal short-sequence representation. However, their linear complexity enables larger batch sizes or longer training runs within the same compute budget, potentially offsetting representational differences. The architectural inductive biases — locality in Longformer, randomised connectivity in BigBird — may act as regularisers that help or hinder depending on task characteristics.

Task 2: Long-Document Understanding

Long-document understanding tasks — such as document classification, question answering over full papers, or multi-document reasoning — require processing sequences far exceeding 512 tokens. BERT cannot natively handle such inputs without truncation, sliding windows with aggregation, or hierarchical encoding, all of which lose cross-segment interactions. Longformer's architecture directly addresses this by scaling linearly with sequence length. Its global attention tokens can be placed at strategic positions (e.g., section headers, [CLS]) to maintain document-level context while local windows capture intra-segment structure. This design aligns with the hierarchical nature of documents, where local coherence coexists with global discourse structure.
BigBird's sparse attention provides an alternative approach to long documents. The combination of local, global, and random attention ensures that information can propagate across the full sequence in few layers, with theoretical guarantees on expressiveness. The random attention component creates shortcut connections that may capture non-local dependencies not aligned with document structure, such as coreference links or thematic recurrence. This can be advantageous for tasks requiring reasoning across distant sections. However, the stochastic attention pattern varies across forward passes, which may complicate interpretability and require more training steps to converge. Both Longformer and BigBird enable end-to-end processing of long documents without external memory or hierarchical modules, representing a significant architectural advance over BERT for this task class.

Discussion

The comparison reveals a spectrum of attention design philosophies. BERT represents the expressiveness extreme: maximum connectivity at quadratic cost. Longformer adopts a structured sparsity approach, leveraging the inductive bias that language exhibits strong locality with sparse global anchors. BigBird pursues theoretical completeness through randomised sparse connectivity, trading determinism for approximation guarantees. For standard-length tasks, BERT's full attention remains a strong baseline, though sparse variants offer computational advantages that may enable more extensive pre-training. For long documents, both Longformer and BigBird overcome BERT's hard length limit, but with different trade-offs: Longformer's deterministic pattern offers predictability and alignment with document structure, while BigBird's random attention provides theoretical expressiveness guarantees at the cost of stochastic variability.
A key consideration is the interaction between attention pattern and pre-training objective. Masked language modelling with sparse attention changes which token pairs can provide gradient signals to each other, potentially altering the learned representations. Longformer's global tokens receive dense supervision from all positions, which may concentrate representational capacity on those tokens. BigBird's random attention distributes supervision more broadly but with variance. These pre-training dynamics affect downstream transfer in ways not fully captured by attention pattern analysis alone. Furthermore, hardware efficiency depends on implementation: blocked sparse matrices for Longformer's local windows map well to GPU kernels, while BigBird's random pattern may require gather-scatter operations that are less efficient despite identical asymptotic complexity.

Conclusion

BERT, Longformer, and BigBird embody three distinct resolutions to the attention scalability problem. BERT accepts quadratic cost for maximal expressiveness within a fixed length. Longformer imposes structured sparsity with global routing for linear scaling and document-aware design. BigBird employs randomised sparse attention with theoretical guarantees for universal approximation. For standard-length classification, BERT's full attention remains competitive, though sparse variants offer training efficiency gains. For long-document understanding, both Longformer and BigBird enable native long-sequence processing, with Longformer favouring structural alignment and BigBird favouring theoretical completeness. The choice among them should be guided by sequence length requirements, task structure, computational constraints, and the availability of domain-adapted pre-trained checkpoints rather than by asymptotic complexity alone.
